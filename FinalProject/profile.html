Exciting Seaweed

An important part of marine ecosystems

Some Types

Splendid Iridescent Seaweed (Mazzaella splendens)

Iridescent Seaweed can be found along the Pacific Coast and can be different colors including brown, green, dark purple, and black. Underwater the seaweed appears iridescent due to a layer of interference. It has thin blades that can grow up to 3 feet long and 10 inches wide and it's texture is smooth and slippery. It is also a source of carrageenan, which is extracted and used to jell, thicken, or stabilize products such as toothpaste, ice cream, and cosmetics.

Sources: Limpets

Red Comb Weed (Plocamium cartilagineum)

Red comb weed is made up of narrow, cartilaginous red fronds that grow up to 300 milimeters long. It branches irregularly and has a spur on each end. It can be found on rocks and Laminaria hyperborea stripes in intertidal and subtidal zones.

Sources: The Seaweed Site AlgaeBase

Sea Lettuce (Ulva lactuca)

Sea lettuce can be found along the Pacific Coast from Alaska to Mexico in bays, lagoons, harbors, marshes, and rocky shores. It is only two cells thick and as a result is almost transparent and can be torn easily. This is an edible type of seaweed and is commonly eaten dried, fresh, or toasted. They are also one of the first algae to grow back after a disturbance so they can help indicate high disturbance areas.

Sources: Limpets

Turkish Towel (Chondracanthus exasperatus)

Turkish towels are found from Alaska to Baja California in rocky intertidal zones. It gets its name from its rough towel-like texture. It faces threats from invasive species such as Mazzaella japonica and from the parasite Pythium marinum. Many predators also prefer not to eat it since it can harm them like it does with a juvenile white abalone, who when they eat too much of the seaweed it can decrease their shell length.

Sources: iNaturalist

Nori (Porphyra spp.)

Nori is commonly found on rocks and man-made structures from Alaska to baja California. It is also only one or two cell layers thick and has thin ruffled blades that vary in color, some of which include green, brown, and black. Since it is a good source of vitamin C, high in iron, and contains lots of protein it is commonly found in food. Japanese nori is cultivated and pressed into sheets that are used for sushi and other dishes.

Sources: Limpets

Tar Spot Algae (Ralfsia spp.)

Tar spot algae grows on rocks in the intertidal zone from Alaska to Mexico. It grows as a smooth black or dark brown crust on the rocks. Since this algae is flat on the rocks and can't be torn out easily, it is more resistant to trampling, pollution, and grazing, so it can become a dominant species.

Sources: Limpets

Dead Man's Fingers (Codium fragile)

Dead man's fingers is made up of spongy, cylindrical branches that are connected by a disk at the base. It's color ranges from dark green to blackish-green and can grow up to 10-40 centimeters in length. It is found in rocky areas of intertidal zones and subtidal zones of open coastal areas. Since a subspecies of it was introduced in the New England coast in 1957, it has become a nuisance to the shellfish industry because it attaches to shells and makes them float away.

Sources: Limpets

Green Pin-Cushion Alga (Cladophora columbiana)

Green pin-cushion alga looks like moss and is made of branched filaments that make up densely matted tufts of bright green. Its matted texture is effective at holding water and can survive long periods without water. It is common in the mid to high intertidal zone from British Colombia to Baja California. Like many other algae species, it competes for space on the rocks and sometimes it will even grow over mussels and barnacles to get more space.

Sources: Limpets
