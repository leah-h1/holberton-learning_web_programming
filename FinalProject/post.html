Wildlife in the Mudflats

Beneath the mud live a vast array of organisms

What Lives There?

Thick-Horned Nudibranch (Hermissenda crassicornis)

The thick-horned nudibranch, also called the opalescent nudibranch, is a species of nudibranch that can be found from Alaska to Northern California. They are quite small and only grow to about 8 centimeters. They can be found in many different habitat types, including mudflats, eelgrass beds, and rocky shores. They feed on sea anemones, ascidians, and hydroids. It has also been observed that they fight with each other and often the winner will eat the loser.

Sources: iNaturalist Biodiversity of the Central Coast

Pacific Gaper Clam (Tresus nuttallii)

The pacific gaper clam is the largest clam in California and can be found in habitats such as mudflats, bays, beaches, and estuaries. They feed on plankton and food that they filter from the water. In the mudflats they can be found by looking for two holes near each other which are the exits to the tunnel that a clams hides in. They can get up to 10 inches in length and live up to 17 years.

Sources: CDFW

Black Lugworm (Arenicola defodiens)

Black lugworms have a thicker upper body that has bristles, known as chaetae, thirteen of which also have gills. They also have a thinner lower body which they can regrow. This type of lugworm varies in color from dark green and brown to black. They are larger than the similar species of blow lugworms, and can grow 40 centimeters or more. They live in J-shaped burrows in the lowest reaches of low tide as well as in mudflats. Lugworms are also frequently used as bait for fish.

Sources: PlanetSeaFishing
